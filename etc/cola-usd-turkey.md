# The case for a cost-of-living adjustment for USD-paid engineers in Turkey

**Engineers paid a fixed USD salary in Turkey since January 2023 have lost approximately 24% of their real purchasing power** — despite the dollar's dramatic appreciation against the Turkish lira. This paradox arises because Turkey's cumulative consumer price inflation of **206%** has vastly outpaced the lira's **134%** depreciation against the dollar. Over the same three-year period, US inflation totaled just **8.7%**, meaning a US-based colleague's purchasing power eroded roughly three cents on the dollar while Turkey-based teammates lost nearly a quarter of theirs. Turkish employers, by comparison, have responded with **40–70% annual salary increases** in the tech sector. A team receiving zero adjustment since January 2023 has effectively taken a steep, compounding pay cut every single year.

---

## Turkey's inflation has been 10–25 times worse than America's

To understand why a USD salary loses value in Turkey, the scale of Turkey's inflation crisis must be grasped. The US experienced painful inflation in 2022–2023, peaking at **9.1%** in June 2022 — the worst in four decades. Turkey's official inflation peaked at **85.5%** in October 2022 and remained above **30%** through January 2026. These are official figures from TÜİK, the Turkish Statistical Institute. Independent researchers at ENAG (Inflation Research Group), a consortium of Turkish university economists, have consistently measured inflation at **1.8–2.3 times** the official rate.

The following table presents the trajectory side by side:

| Period | Turkey CPI (TÜİK, YoY) | US CPI (BLS, YoY) | Turkey-to-US ratio |
|--------|------------------------|--------------------|-------------------|
| Jan 2023 | 57.68% | 6.4% | 9x |
| Jun 2023 | 38.21% | 3.0% | 13x |
| Dec 2023 | 64.77% | 3.4% | 19x |
| May 2024 (Turkey peak) | **75.45%** | 3.3% | **23x** |
| Dec 2024 | 44.38% | 2.9% | 15x |
| Dec 2025 | 30.89% | 2.7% | 11x |
| Jan 2026 (latest) | **30.65%** | **2.4%** | **13x** |

**Cumulative inflation from January 2023 to January 2026** compounds as follows: Turkey's official CPI rose by a factor of **3.06x** (206%), calculated by compounding the January-over-January rates of 64.86%, 42.12%, and 30.65%. The US CPI index moved from approximately 299.1 to 325.3, a cumulative increase of just **8.7%**. By ENAG's independent measurement, Turkey's cumulative price increase over roughly the same window approaches **559%** — meaning real-world prices may have risen **6.6 times**, not merely tripled.

For an American frame of reference: when US grocery bills rose 10–15%, it dominated headlines and shaped a presidential election. Turkish grocery bills rose **70% in a single year**, and this repeated year after year. US rent increases of 8% were considered a crisis; Turkish rent inflation exceeded **121%** annually at its peak in 2024, and was still running at **83%** year-over-year in mid-2025. Turkey's central bank raised its policy rate to **50%** — nearly ten times the US Federal Reserve's peak of 5.5% — reflecting the severity of the crisis.

## The dollar buys more lira but less life in Turkey

The most critical data point in this document is the **purchasing power gap** between exchange rate movement and domestic inflation. This is the mechanism by which a frozen USD salary erodes in real terms, and it is counterintuitive enough to require careful explanation.

**Exchange rate movement:** In January 2023, one US dollar bought **18.70 Turkish lira**. By February 2026, one dollar buys approximately **43.76 lira** — a **134% increase** in lira terms. On the surface, this looks like a windfall for USD earners.

**But prices rose faster than the lira fell.** A basket of goods and services costing 18,700 TL in January 2023 now costs approximately **57,259 TL** (18,700 × 3.062) based on official CPI data. Converting $1,000 at today's rate yields only **43,760 TL** — a shortfall of **13,499 TL**, or **23.6% less purchasing power** than the same $1,000 provided three years ago.

| | January 2023 | February 2026 |
|--|-------------|----------------|
| Salary (USD) | $1,000 | $1,000 (no raise) |
| Exchange rate | 18.70 TRY/$ | 43.76 TRY/$ |
| TRY received | 18,700 TL | 43,760 TL |
| Cost of Jan 2023 basket today | — | 57,259 TL |
| **Purchasing power gap** | — | **-23.6%** |

The formula is straightforward: **Real purchasing power change = (1 + exchange rate gain) ÷ (1 + cumulative inflation) − 1**, which yields (2.340 ÷ 3.062) − 1 = **−23.6%**. Using ENAG's independent inflation estimates, the loss would be far steeper — potentially exceeding **50%**.

This happens because Turkey has one of the highest exchange-rate pass-through rates among emerging markets. When the lira falls, import costs spike immediately (Turkey imports **93% of its oil** and **99% of its natural gas**), feeding into transportation, food production, and energy costs. Simultaneously, backward-looking indexation in rents, contracts, and administered prices locks in past inflation, while wage-price spirals from aggressive minimum wage increases push service costs higher. The result: domestic prices consistently outrun currency depreciation.

A concrete illustration: in January 2023, $1,000 converted to lira could cover approximately **23 months** of basic groceries for one person in a major Turkish city (at roughly 800 TL/month). Today, with groceries running approximately 2,500 TL/month, that same $1,000 covers only about **17.5 months** — a **24% reduction** in grocery purchasing power, precisely matching the macro calculation.

## Operating costs for sole proprietorships have tripled

Turkish law requires freelancers and remote contractors working for foreign companies to operate through a **şahıs şirketi** (sole proprietorship). This is not optional — it is a legal mandate that creates substantial overhead costs that have exploded alongside inflation.

**Social security premiums (Bağ-Kur)** are the largest mandatory cost. Sole proprietors pay approximately 34.5–35.5% of the gross minimum wage as their monthly premium. Because the minimum wage has risen from 10,008 TL to 33,030 TL, annual Bağ-Kur costs have surged from roughly **42,000 TL in 2023 to 121,000 TL in 2026** — a **188% increase**. A new 2026 law (Law 7566) also eliminated the first-year Bağ-Kur exemption for young entrepreneurs, adding an immediate burden for newer contractors.

**Accountant fees** are mandatory, as sole proprietors must retain a certified public accountant (SMMM). Market rates in Istanbul have risen from roughly 700–1,500 TL/month in 2023 to **3,500–6,000 TL/month in 2026** — an increase of **300% or more**. Declaration stamp taxes, which must be paid on every monthly and quarterly filing, have risen from approximately 3,000–4,000 TL/year to **17,120 TL/year in 2026** — a roughly **400% increase**.

The minimum monthly "floor cost" to simply operate a sole proprietorship in 2026 — before earning a single lira of take-home pay — is approximately **17,000–18,000 TL/month (~$390–415 USD)**, covering Bağ-Kur, accountant, virtual office, and filing fees. In 2023, this floor was roughly 5,500–6,500 TL/month (~$230–275 USD).

Turkey's progressive income tax system adds further burden. The 2026 non-wage income brackets are:

| Taxable income (TRY) | Rate |
|---|---|
| 0 – 190,000 | 15% |
| 190,001 – 400,000 | 20% |
| 400,001 – 1,000,000 | 27% |
| 1,000,001 – 5,300,000 | 35% |
| Over 5,300,000 | 40% |

For a contractor earning $4,000/month, the **total tax and cost burden** (income tax + Bağ-Kur + accountant + all fees) consumes approximately **35–36% of gross income** in both 2023 and 2026. While the percentage has remained relatively stable, the absolute amounts in lira have roughly tripled. More critically, the after-tax take-home in USD has actually **slightly decreased** — from approximately $31,380/year in 2023 to $30,913/year in 2026 for a $48,000 gross — because bracket adjustments have not fully kept pace with the exchange rate shift.

## Turkish tech employers have given 40–70% annual raises

Perhaps the most compelling benchmark for a COLA request is what **Turkish employers themselves** have done to retain engineering talent. The data is stark: while these remote engineers received zero adjustment, the domestic market responded aggressively to inflation.

**Turkey's minimum wage** — the baseline reference for all salary negotiations — increased from **10,008 TL gross in January 2023 to 33,030 TL gross in January 2026**, a cumulative increase of **230%**. This required two adjustments in 2023 alone (January and July), reflecting the government's acknowledgment that wages must track inflation or face social instability.

Tech sector salary increases have tracked at or above minimum wage adjustments:

| Year | Minimum wage increase | Estimated tech sector raises | Official avg CPI |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2023 | ~100% (two adjustments) | 40–70% | 53.86% |
| 2024 | 49% | 35–55% | 58.51% |
| 2025 | 30% | 25–40% | ~35% |
| 2026 | 27% | 20–30% (projected) | 16% target |

According to a 2025 verified salary guide, mid-level software developers in Turkey now earn **60,000–95,000 TL net per month** (up from an estimated 30,000–50,000 TL in early 2023), and senior developers earn **95,000–150,000+ TL net per month**. ECA International's 2025–2026 salary trends survey, covering 200 multinationals, projects Turkey will record the **highest real salary growth in Europe in 2026 at 8.1%**, though the report notes "Turks are still a long way behind the buying power they used to have."

The emergence of a **two-tier labor market** is particularly relevant. Turkish developers working remotely for US and EU companies earn **2–5 times more** than peers at local firms, with average remote developer compensation around **$56,600–$120,000/year**. This premium exists precisely because global employers recognize the need to compensate for Turkey's economic conditions. ICT graduates have the **highest emigration rate** of any field at **6.7%**, with the US (19.6%) and Germany (19.4%) as top destinations — a brain drain driven directly by economic conditions.

## What the economic crisis looks like from the ground

Turkey's crisis stems from an extraordinary monetary experiment. From 2021 to mid-2023, President Erdoğan forced the central bank to **cut interest rates to fight inflation** — the opposite of mainstream economic policy. The policy rate fell to **8.5%** even as inflation surged past 85%. The lira lost 57% of its value in 2021 alone, falling from 7.4 to 17 per dollar. Net reserves dropped to just **$2.33 billion** by May 2023, bringing Turkey to the edge of a balance-of-payments crisis.

A policy reversal in June 2023 brought orthodox economics back — rates were hiked from 8.5% to 50% — but the damage was done. Cumulative price increases had already embedded themselves into rents, wages, and expectations. As of January 2026, inflation remains at **30.65%**, the policy rate sits at **37%**, and the recovery remains fragile. The March 2025 arrest of Istanbul's opposition mayor triggered a **10% lira crash in hours** and forced the central bank to spend over **$40 billion** in reserves in a single month, demonstrating how quickly progress can unravel.

The human toll is severe. **65.7% of all household spending** goes to just food, housing, and transportation — rising to **73.7% for low-income households**. The hunger threshold for a family of four stood at **29,828 TL/month** in late 2025, meaning the net minimum wage of 28,075 TL falls **below** basic food needs. Turkey's fertility rate collapsed from 2.00 in 2018 to **1.48 in 2024**, attributed directly to economic conditions. Landlord-tenant disputes over rent increases have turned violent, with at least **11 people killed** in related incidents.

## Conclusion: what the data supports

The quantitative case for a cost-of-living adjustment is unambiguous across every metric examined. Official Turkish inflation has compounded to **206%** since January 2023 — and independent measurement suggests the true figure may be nearly three times higher. The net purchasing power of a frozen USD salary has declined by **approximately 24%** using conservative official data, despite the dollar more than doubling against the lira. Turkish employers have responded with cumulative tech salary increases of **100–170%** over the same period. Sole proprietorship operating costs have risen **188–400%** depending on the category, with the minimum monthly floor cost to simply maintain a legal business nearly doubling in dollar terms.

Three specific findings deserve emphasis. First, the purchasing power gap is **structural, not temporary** — Turkey's high exchange-rate pass-through, backward-looking price indexation, and import dependency mean domestic prices will continue outrunning lira depreciation for years. Second, the **sole proprietorship tax and cost burden** of 33–37% of gross income is a hidden cost that US managers may not realize their contractors bear. Third, the **brain drain data** — with ICT having the highest emigration rate of any field — signals that retention risk is real and growing. A COLA adjustment of at least **25–30%** based on official data (or significantly more using independent inflation estimates) would bring these engineers back to the purchasing power they had when hired, while remaining well below the **100–170% cumulative raises** that Turkish domestic employers have provided to retain comparable talent.
